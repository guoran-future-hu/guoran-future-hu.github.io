Institutions like ours regularly invite industry leaders to speak about their experiences. One day it might be Dana White from the UFC; the next, the CEO of a public company; the day after, the creator of *Angry Birds*.

In principle, absorbing perspectives from different domains is a good thing. The problem is that I rarely extract information that is directly *useful* to me.

The reason is straightforward. Success factors are largely non-reproducible and statistically intractable. When industry leaders retrospectively narrate their trajectories, they tend to distill a few factors they consider “critical,” or emphasize mistakes they believe must be avoided. But upon closer inspection, these summaries are nothing more than **single-perspective observations of an extremely high-dimensional problem**.

<br>

# A Brief Digression On The Placebo Effect…

Many illnesses have strong endogenous components. Simply *believing* that one is taking medication—even when one consciously knows it is a placebo—can still lead to real symptom improvement through unconscious psychological mechanisms, despite the absence of any active drug.

Before the invention of the microscope, most traditional medical systems—tribal shamanism, early Western medicine with its fixation on bloodletting, and other herb-based pre-scientific medical traditions—can be understood, to a large extent, as attempts to harness the placebo effect.

In much the same way, most “success stories” and industry experience sharing are essentially personal narratives shaped by survivorship bias.

The parallel is this: before we have a “microscope,” we are dealing with a highly complex black-box system. Individuals construct explanations from their own experiences and vantage points, but those explanations do not establish causality. At best, they reflect statistical correlations.

Without a traceable causal chain, any “methodology” abstracted from such experiences is inherently unreliable.

<br>

# Another View on Causality

More than two thousand years ago, Plato described a similar epistemic problem in the Allegory of the Cave: prisoners take shadows on a wall to be reality, unaware that they are only projections.

More broadly, many systems in the world are black boxes by nature. Some are simple; others are so complex that they push beyond the limits of human intuition.

For simple systems, the brain—remarkably good at pattern recognition—can understand them quickly. But once a system becomes sufficiently abstract or complex, intuition begins to fail. At that point, we either construct an explanatory framework to make sense of it, or, more lazily, we mistake a few observed correlations for causation and declare the problem solved.

After Plato, Hume made an even more radical claim in the 18th century: humans cannot directly perceive causality. What we actually observe are events—and the repeated co-occurrence of events. **Causation itself is not something we see in the world; it is a mental construct we use to make the world intelligible.**

When a correlation appears frequently and consistently enough, we internalize a rule: if A happens, B follows.

At the time, this was scandalous. But ask yourself—have you ever actually *seen* causality? You have only observed that “people die when killed” a hundred times without exception, and so you assume the 101st case will unfold the same way.

If you’re familiar with AI, the parallel is hard to miss. GPT can solve math problems not because it perceives mathematical causality, but because it has absorbed sufficiently stable patterns from vast amounts of data.

From this angle, causality, logic, and even mathematics are explanatory frameworks humans devised to compress and describe recurring patterns in experience.

**The “world engine” itself is indifferent. It simply runs according to its own rules—a vast black box.**

Any human model imposed on reality inevitably introduces error. Newtonian mechanics breaks down at Mercury’s perihelion; relativity fails at quantum scales; planned economies fail to align with individual demand. In the same way, most success narratives fail to reproduce success.

Interestingly, a similar intuition appears in the ancient East. Laozi wrote, “The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao.” The underlying structure of reality, once put into words, is already simplified—already distorted.

Two thousand years later, Wittgenstein arrived at a similar conclusion: “Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.” The deepest structure of the world lies beyond the limits of language and formal logic.

Across centuries and cultures, the convergence is striking.

<br>

This also helps explain why early symbolic and logic-based AI failed, while connectionism and neural networks succeeded.

We stopped forcing AI to understand the world in ways we considered formally “correct.” Instead, we allowed it to learn directly from data—from phenomena themselves. When correlations accumulate at sufficient scale, structures that resemble causality begin to emerge on their own.

That shift may be one of the most important methodological lessons AI has offered back to science. Interested in this topic? I’ll write a blog about it someday!

# Systems, Luck, and Environment

Returning to industry experience sharing.

Much of it rests on methodological individualism: success is attributed to personality traits or a handful of “key decisions.” In reality, markets move in cycles. During expansion, almost anything yields returns; during contraction, a minor mistake is enough for assets to be “re-priced by the invisible hand” down to the level of one’s actual understanding.

History shows similar dynamics. In medieval Europe, when life expectancy was low and social mobility limited, how many elites were born into noble families? And in chaotic periods like the collapse of the Western Roman Empire—essentially a prolonged historical free-for-all—the most rational life strategy often reduced to being prepared to “reroll your game character” at any moment.

In recent years, my own view has shifted toward system-level structuralism. Just as the motion of a single atom is highly unpredictable, individual trajectories are shaped by randomness within larger historical currents. “Success” often means being in the right place at the right time, moving with the flow of social resources, and making a few decisions that are not catastrophic.

Environment matters.

Luck is part of the environment—one that individualist narratives consistently downplay.

A [Veritasium video](https://youtu.be/3LopI4YeC4I?si=S9eZxxe8VFFMAFTQ) illustrates this with a simple model: even if luck contributes to only 10% of outcomes, nearly all top performers end up with a disproportionately favorable share of it.

If you extend the idea of environment to its logical conclusion, you arrive at something blunt but useful: stop listening to “if they can do it, why can’t you?” Everyone begins with different initial conditions, follows different paths, and faces a uniquely configured environment at any given moment.

People with limited physical capacity or chronic mental illness operate under higher baseline costs. Rather than forcing yourself to match someone else’s productivity, it is more rational to optimize the constraints of your own environment first.

Even if you try to create opportunities from nothing, luck remains an irreducible stochastic term—something like quantum fluctuations.

So don’t overestimate individual agency. And don’t impose pressure on yourself based on models that ignore the structure of the system.

# Conclusion

What, then, is the conclusion about industry experience sharing?

You should still listen.

Yes, these talks are fragmented observations of a black box. But that doesn’t make them useless. It simply changes how you use them. You collect as many accounts as possible—like training data for a neural network—and look for patterns that remain stable across different contexts.

The aim is to gradually build an internal model of how the world works. You can call it causality, industry intuition, or simply a world model.

The conclusion itself hasn’t changed. What matters is the mode of thinking. You train yourself to break phenomena down to their smallest components—to reason from **first principles**—and then reconstruct understanding from the ground up.

I think this is one of the most important **general-purpose skills**: the ability to spot patterns in noisy reality and pull something reliable out of loose correlations.

In the post-AI era—especially as AI systems move steadily toward human-level competence—this ability may become even more valuable. As specialized tasks are increasingly automated, the advantage will shift toward people who can navigate complex systems, integrate information across domains, and build robust mental models of the world.

That brings us to the next question: **what kinds of skills actually remain valuable in such an environment?**
