Earlier I mentioned that I’m not particularly satisfied with the *information density of genuinely high-value insights* in the talks organized by my institution. These events tend to transmit highly fragmented information, which I then have to digest and integrate on my own.

That’s not to say that internalizing fragmented inputs and extracting patterns isn’t an important capability.

Nor is it to say that these events are bad—I may simply be lazy.

So the question becomes: are there learning channels with higher information density?

<br>

# Mechanism-Level Thinking

Setting books aside for a moment, there are two people whose talk I genuinely enjoy listening to—and whose every sentence I tend to take seriously.

The first is my undergraduate cognitive science professor, John Vervaeke. He has a roughly fifty-hour [podcast series](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLND1JCRq8Vuh3f0P5qjrSdb5eC1ZfZwWJ) that attempts to break down a highly complex, interdisciplinary social problem layer by layer, analyze its underlying mechanisms, and construct a relatively unified explanatory framework. It’s extremely insightful.

The second is—surprisingly—a suggestive streamer who discusses politics, history, current affairs, economics… literally anything.

These two share a common trait. When they talk about an issue, they’re not merely recounting things they’ve observed as individuals. They consistently try to abstract the underlying mechanisms that govern how things work.

Many educational content creators do this to some extent. So why single these two out?

Their arguments tend to be *surgically precise*. Their arguments are close to first principles, connect multiple domains, explain a lot of things. and once you listen, you immediately feel a deep coherence in a way that clicks.

Not everyone naturally operates at that level of abstraction. It seems to require a particular combination of cognitive habits, interdisciplinary exposure, and long periods of thinking about mechanisms rather than surface observations.

Interestingly, both of them seem to share a similar cognitive style: a strong inclination toward abstraction and mechanism-level thinking.

I used to think that people like us simply enjoy this kind of geeky abstraction for its own sake. But over time I realized that the ability to extract fundamental, world-level mechanisms from everyday phenomena is an exceptionally rare and valuable skill.

Even setting natural tendencies aside, cultivating this ability still requires enormous amounts of reading and sustained thinking. The same pattern appears in other domains: some people may have a natural inclination toward empathy, but providing genuine emotional support—comforting others, building trust, and offering meaningful guidance—is itself a demanding skill that only emerges through extensive interpersonal experience.

These, incidentally, are two examples of what I now strongly advocate as **general-purpose skills**.

<br>

# **General-purpose skills**

Over the past few years, we’ve been busy debating who gets to define “artificial general intelligence” and criticizing AI as merely a collection of narrow, task-specific tools. But have we stopped to consider that in a highly specialized, efficiency-obsessed society, the roles society expects us to play are themselves quite specialized? How many people can confidently say they are broadly capable, well-rounded human beings with general skills?

> A Man should be able to change a diaper, plan an invasion, butcher a hog, conn a ship, design a building, write a sonnet, balance accounts, build a wall, set a bone, comfort the dying, take orders, give orders, cooperate, act alone, solve equations, analyze a new problem, pitch manure, program a computer, cook a tasty meal, fight efficiently and die gallantly. Specialization is for insects.
> 

— Robert A. Heinlein, *Time Enough for Love*

<br>

The development of AI has been unexpected. Few would have predicted that AI could barely do anything before 2022, yet all of a sudden, they almost mastered the entire human language skill.

Based on the trajectory of the past few years, one inference stands out rather clearly: **the more specialized and vertically constrained a skill is, the easier it is for AI to automate; the more general and cross-contextual a skill is, the harder it is for AI to fully replace**.

This is not to say that specialization is simple and generality inherently difficult. Mathematical proof is highly specialized; running a local convenience store is highly general.

Yet many jobs people work in their professional lives are far less general than they appear.

<br>

Over the coming decades, humanity will likely face several questions. Will there be a point at which AI’s aggregate capabilities comprehensively surpass those of humans? Will there be a point at which AI can recursively improve itself, leading to exponential intelligence growth?

In the worst case, this would constitute an existential crisis: a “singularity” in which machine intelligence iterates beyond human control and dominance.

But let’s bracket science fiction for now—not because it’s impossible, but because I want to talk about something that is closer to us. Let’s assume the best case: human civilization continues to flourish, with AI dramatically boosting productivity.

History shows that major advances in productive forces have never merely “made the old world run faster.” They inevitably force societies to redefine the meaning of labor, the structure of division of work, and the role of individuals within the system. From agriculture to industry, from handicraft to mechanized production, the restructuring of production relations has never been optional—it has always lagged behind changes in productivity as a necessity.

As highly vertical jobs become automated, the division of labor will inevitably shift. Even under optimistic assumptions, **changes in productive forces and production relations will fundamentally reshape social structures and interpersonal relationships**.

When machines become sufficiently human-like, will some people want to form romantic relationships with them? At the very least, people are already using LLMs as a substitute therapist.

What many fail to notice is that in the age of the internet and short-form media, the relationship between capital productivity and labor described in Marx’s *Capital* no longer fully applies. The theoretical models of that era struggle to explain phenomena that now feel mundane—such as virtual assets and digital platforms.

A “soft” singularity is still a singularity. Its pace may be slower, but it will still outstrip every industrial revolution in human history.

<br>

The endlessly studied question of “how to become successful” is, in reality, highly contingent on the prevailing social environment and production relations.

A strategy that built fortunes in one era may fail completely in another. The industrialists of the early twentieth century, the personal-computing pioneers of the 1980s, and the internet entrepreneurs of the 2000s all operated under radically different technological and economic conditions.

This resembles evolution: a species optimally adapted to one environment and era may fail catastrophically in another.

Sinisphere traditions like “having no descendants is the greatest unfilial act” or “start a family before building a career” were well-suited to village-based agrarian economies. In an industrial society emphasizing occupational specialization, they become traps, because the time required to raise a child to economic productivity has increased dramatically.

Similarly, slogans like “master STEM, and you’ll succeed anywhere” reflect an industrial-era belief in specialization as competitive traits.

But as AI replaces more specialized roles and production relations begin to shift, do these familiar doctrines still hold? What skills are truly adaptive to the next era?

This finally brings us back to general-purpose skills.

If you were to interview AI researchers and ask which capabilities AI is least likely to achieve, the answers would converge on the most complex, general, coordination-heavy human skills.

These typically include: **emotional support and companionship; building trust and social connection; identifying market needs and designing, operating products; coordinating people and resources; extracting latent patterns from observed phenomena; macro-level judgment and strategic decision-making; and adaptability to changing environments**.

These “non-instrumental” tasks share a common feature: they emphasize interaction with the world and with other people, and they depend on how one understands the world’s dynamics—what AI research now commonly refers to as a **world model**.

A “world model,” put simply, is an internal representation of how the world operates: who interacts with whom, which actions produce which consequences, and under what conditions environments change.

Earlier generations of AI lacked this entirely. The most advanced systems simply emitted the statistically most probable next token.

Today’s AI, however, has begun to acquire simple world models. As they start to internalize the logic of how the world works, they are moving toward three-dimensional space and physical interaction, and toward performing more complex, general tasks.

Meanwhile, we—who pride ourselves on being the more intelligent species—rarely acknowledge that the educational and career pathways designed for us barely touch this higher layer of world modeling.

This massive social machine, optimized for efficiency, does not require its components to understand anything beyond their assigned scope.

As AI becomes capable of handling more “instrumental” tasks, humans can operate at higher, more abstract levels.

In fact, “can” is a bit of a conservative word. Humans will be forced to learn more complex general-purpose skills; otherwise, we will be competing directly with AI in domains where human efficiency cannot possibly match machine’s.

I know this may sound alarmist. But leaders in the AI industry—including our distinguished alumnus Sam Altman—have repeatedly expressed similar views over the past year. Read the full interview in [this Politico article](https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2025/09/25/sam-altman-ai-interview-axel-springer-00580997).

> **Q:** You became a father this year. What education would you advise your son to pursue so that his job won’t simply be replaced by AI in 30 years?
>
> **A:** The meta-skill of learning how to learn, of learning to adapt, learning to be resilient to a lot of change. Learning how to figure out what people want, how to make useful products and services for them, how to interact in the world.

Unfortunately, I didn’t arrive at this conclusion before Altman did. I’ll try harder next time.

<br>

As he suggests, the next generation should prioritize these general-purpose skills, rather than STEM subjects and hyper-specialization that our era still glorifies.

The pace of AI development is difficult to forecast. This shift may arrive sooner than expected—possibly before our own generation reaches retirement.

<br>

There is another factor at play, unrelated to AI but deeply tied to our era.

In most cases, **the returns to specialization depend on environmental and rule stability, making it an efficiency-optimal strategy. But when industries reshuffle and order breaks down, over-specialization often leads to a loss of agency and resilience**.

This pattern appears in every technological revolution and industrial transition. A portion of the workforce always loses their jobs and is eventually absorbed into low-value-added service sectors.

The current international order increasingly resembles a reshuffle. China is extending its strategic reach westward; the U.S. is retreating from global hegemony toward isolationism; Europe continues to struggle with economic and political pressures.; Russia has been worn down by prolonged conflict; and the Gulf states—led by the UAE—are positioning themselves as a new technology and financial hub at the crossroads of Asia, Africa, and Europe.

(Update in 2026 March: A war in the Middle East has thrown the region into chaos. See, reshuffles!)

And this restructuring is not confined to geopolitics.

The traditional definition of sovereignty includes not only territory and military force, but also rule-making authority and infrastructure control. Internet giants like Google and Meta, which control platforms, compute, and information channels, now participate de facto in rule-setting, information distribution, and cognitive shaping—functions once exclusive to states.

In regions lacking regulatory capacity or digital infrastructure, entire segments of economic life depend on platforms. In extreme cases, tech giants can bypass or hollow out governmental authority.

Hobbes described the state as a Leviathan embodying sovereign power. Today’s platform-based, data-driven conglomerates resemble a new kind of **Digital Leviathan**.

And these Leviathans also control AI models and compute that may reshape the next generation of productive forces and production relations.

# Conclusion

The wave of change is approaching. The more unstable the environment becomes, the more systems favor individuals with **transferable cognitive abilities, cross-domain abstraction capacity, resource integration skills, and the ability to build interpersonal trust**.

On the eve of shifts in geopolitics, industrial structure, and production relations, doubling down on specialization and chasing stability only amplifies future risk and uncertainty.

Yet contemporary society—including educational systems and career pathways—remains wholly unprepared for this structural transition.

We used to obsess over which specific jobs AI would replace. The more pressing question now is: once components are replaced, where do the components go? After spending half a lifetime grinding out a competitive skill in a vertical domain, will one day’s AI tsunami simply wash it all away?

In the post-AI era, general-purpose skills will no longer be optional upgrades for personal growth; they will be the core means of retaining agency.

Those who adapt fast will stabilize first in chaos—and will be best positioned to claim first-mover advantage in whatever new frontier emerges.
